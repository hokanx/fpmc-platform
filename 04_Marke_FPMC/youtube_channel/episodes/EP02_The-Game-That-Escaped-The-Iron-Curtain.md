## Sources (verified 2026-07)

- Alexey Pajitnov, Soviet Academy of Sciences computing center, Moscow, 1984; Electronika 60 (text-only display); name = tetra + tennis
- Vadim Gerasimov (16) ported to IBM PC with color; spread via copied floppies
- Robert Stein (Andromeda) saw it in Budapest, licensed onward to Mirrorsoft (Maxwell) / Spectrum HoloByte **before** owning rights
- Feb 1989 Moscow: Henk Rogers, Robert Stein, Kevin Maxwell negotiate separately with ELORG; Stein's contract never covered handhelds
- Rogers + Nintendo obtain handheld/console rights; Game Boy (1989) bundles Tetris → ~35M copies
- Pajitnov earned no royalties until rights reverted in 1996 → The Tetris Company (with Rogers); emigrated to the US in 1991

## Narration blocks

```
Block 1
This is the most successful video game of all time. It was born behind the Iron Curtain, and its creator earned nothing for twelve years.
Block 2
It escaped Moscow on copied floppy disks, triggered a corporate war, and put three desperate men on planes to the Soviet Union.
Block 3
This is the true story of Tetris — the puzzle that outlived an empire.
Block 4
Moscow, nineteen eighty-four. Deep inside the Soviet Academy of Sciences, a researcher named Alexey Pajitnov is supposed to be testing hardware.
Block 5
Instead, he is thinking about pentominoes, a childhood puzzle of wooden shapes. What if the pieces fell from above, one by one?
Block 6
His computer, an Electronika sixty, cannot even draw graphics. So he builds his falling shapes out of brackets and text characters.
Block 7
He simplifies five-square pieces down to four. Tetra, for four. Tennis, his favorite sport. He names the file Tetris.
Block 8
Something strange happens. Pajitnov cannot stop playing his own unfinished game. Neither can anyone else who touches his keyboard.
Block 9
Within weeks the game has silently conquered the institute. Productivity collapses, one falling block at a time.
Block 10
A sixteen-year-old colleague, Vadim Gerasimov, ports it to the IBM PC, adds color, and the game slips out on hand-copied floppy disks.
Block 11
There is no marketing, no company, no price tag. Tetris spreads across Moscow, then Eastern Europe, like a rumor.
Block 12
In Budapest, a British software dealer named Robert Stein sees it running on a screen, and instantly smells money.
Block 13
Stein telexes Moscow asking for the rights. Pajitnov sends a friendly, noncommittal reply. Stein reads it as a yes.
Block 14
It is not a yes. But Stein starts selling licenses anyway — to a British media empire, and to an American publisher.
Block 15
By nineteen eighty-eight, Tetris is a Western hit, while the Soviet state, and its creator, have not seen a single dollar.
Block 16
Then the game lands in front of a Dutch-born entrepreneur in Japan named Henk Rogers. He bets his house on it. Literally.
Block 17
Rogers wants Tetris for a secret new Nintendo device — a handheld with a small green screen, called the Game Boy.
Block 18
But the paper trail of rights is a knot of contradictions. In February nineteen eighty-nine, three men fly to Moscow to untangle it.
Block 19
Stein. A representative of the media tycoon Robert Maxwell. And Rogers, who arrives uninvited, on a tourist visa.
Block 20
The Soviets run the talks through Elorg, the state software agency. Its officials discover how little they were ever told.
Block 21
Rogers does something the others never tried. He shows his cards, opens his books, and befriends Alexey Pajitnov.
Block 22
Elorg's verdict is brutal. Stein's contract never covered handhelds at all. The most valuable rights on Earth are still for sale.
Block 23
Rogers and Nintendo sign for the handheld rights. The Maxwell empire rages, pressuring even the Kremlin itself. It changes nothing.
Block 24
The deal of the decade is done — for a game that its own creator still does not own.
Block 25
That summer, the Game Boy launches with Tetris in the box. The puzzle becomes the reason millions buy the machine.
Block 26
Thirty-five million cartridges later, Tetris is a global language. Grandmothers play it. Astronauts play it in orbit.
Block 27
And Alexey Pajitnov? He is still a state employee on a state salary. The Soviet Union is collecting his royalties.
Block 28
In nineteen ninety-one, as the USSR collapses, Pajitnov finally emigrates to America — famous everywhere, and nearly broke.
Block 29
Five more years pass. In nineteen ninety-six the rights come home at last, and with Rogers he founds the Tetris Company.
Block 30
A puzzle born in a locked empire became the most portable idea in gaming history. Next time: the handshake that betrayed Nintendo, and created PlayStation.
```
