# EP03 — The Betrayal That Created PlayStation

**Status:** script-final (narration complete; block prompts derived at

**Style key:** reuse EP01's key job id

## Sources (verified 2026-07)

- Ken Kutaragi designed the Super Famicom sound chip (SPC700) at Sony against internal resistance; president Norio Ohga protected him
- Sony–Nintendo "Play Station" SNES CD add-on partnership, late 1980s
- CES Las Vegas, June 1991: Sony announces the Play Station; the following day Nintendo announces a partnership with Philips instead
- Ohga's "table-pounding" go-ahead for Sony's own console is well documented
- PlayStation launch: Japan Dec 3, 1994; US Sept 1995 at $299 (the famous one-word "299" price reveal)
- First home console to ship 100M+ units; a surviving Nintendo Play Station prototype sold at auction in 2020 for $360,000

## Narration blocks

```
Block 1
The best-selling game console of the nineteen nineties was created by accident — by a betrayal, on a public stage, in front of the world's press.
Block 2
Its maker didn't even want to be in the games business. Until its biggest partner humiliated it, in the space of twenty-four hours.
Block 3
This is the true story of how Nintendo created its own worst enemy: PlayStation.
Block 4
Tokyo, the late nineteen eighties. Nintendo rules the video game world, and Sony is a giant of music, movies, and electronics.
Block 5
Inside Sony, a young engineer named Ken Kutaragi is fascinated by games — an obsession his bosses consider beneath the company.
Block 6
Working quietly, almost secretly, Kutaragi designs the sound chip for Nintendo's new Super Famicom. His superiors are furious.
Block 7
Sony's president, Norio Ohga, overrules them all and protects him. Remember that decision. Everything that follows depends on it.
Block 8
The partnership deepens. Nintendo asks Sony to build a CD-ROM add-on for the Super Nintendo. Its working name: the Play Station.
Block 9
For Kutaragi, this is the dream. Sony would finally enter the games industry through the front door, hand in hand with Nintendo.
Block 10
But inside Nintendo, doubts are growing. The contract gives Sony sweeping control over CD-based games, and the money that follows them.
Block 11
Nintendo's president, Hiroshi Yamauchi, reads the fine print and sees a trap. Sony would not be a partner. It would be a landlord.
Block 12
So Nintendo decides, in absolute secrecy, to smile, shake hands — and quietly negotiate with Sony's rival, Philips.
Block 13
June nineteen ninety-one. The Consumer Electronics Show in Las Vegas. Sony proudly announces the Play Station to the world.
Block 14
The applause is still warm the next morning, when Nintendo takes the same stage — and announces its new partnership with Philips.
Block 15
No warning. No phone call. Sony learns of the betrayal along with everyone else in the room.
Block 16
In Japanese business culture this is more than a broken deal. It is a public loss of face. A declaration of war.
Block 17
Inside Sony, executives want to shut the whole games project down. The humiliation, they argue, should end there.
Block 18
Kutaragi refuses to let it die. He stands before Norio Ohga and asks one question: are we going to sit back and accept this?
Block 19
Ohga's answer becomes legend. He pounds the table and orders Kutaragi to proceed. Sony will build its own machine.
Block 20
The project is exiled to a distant corner of the company, treated as an embarrassment. Kutaragi's small team simply keeps building.
Block 21
They design their console around one bet: three-dimensional graphics on compact discs — cheap to produce, and vast in capacity.
Block 22
While Nintendo stays loyal to expensive cartridges, Sony courts the developers Nintendo squeezed for years. Sony's terms: generous.
Block 23
December third, nineteen ninety-four. The PlayStation launches in Japan, and the first shipment sells out immediately.
Block 24
In nineteen ninety-five it lands in America at two hundred ninety-nine dollars — a price announced on stage as a single word.
Block 25
Games like never before pour onto the platform. Racing, fighting, role-playing epics spanning multiple discs. Cartridges suddenly feel ancient.
Block 26
The numbers become historic. PlayStation becomes the first home console in history to ship more than one hundred million units.
Block 27
Nintendo survives, but its decade of near-total dominance is over — ended by the partner it abandoned at the altar.
Block 28
Ken Kutaragi, once told that games were beneath Sony, becomes known as the father of the PlayStation, and rises to lead the division.
Block 29
And the original hybrid machine? A few prototypes survived. In twenty twenty, one sold at auction for three hundred sixty thousand dollars.
Block 30
Gaming's greatest rivalry wasn't born in a laboratory. It was born from wounded pride, on a stage in Las Vegas. Next time on Playback.
```
